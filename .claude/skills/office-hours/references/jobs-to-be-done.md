# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) Framework

A job is the progress a person seeks in a specific circumstance. The job exists
independent of any solution.

## Structure

**When** {situation}, **I want to** {motivation}, **so I can** {outcome}.

The situation is the trigger — not a demographic, not a persona. The same
person has different jobs in different circumstances.

## Examples

| Wrong (solution-shaped)             | Right (job-shaped)                                                              |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Users want a dashboard"            | "When my deploy finishes, I want to know if it's healthy so I can move on"      |
| "Developers need better logging"    | "When a bug report comes in, I want to see what happened so I can reproduce it" |
| "Teams want notifications"          | "When a teammate blocks on my PR, I want to know quickly so they aren't stuck"  |
| "People want to track their habits" | "When I wake up, I want to see my streak so I stay motivated"                   |

## Key principles

**Compete with non-consumption.** The real competitor is often "do nothing" or
"use a spreadsheet." Ask: what does the user do today without your solution?

**Functional, emotional, social.** Every job has three dimensions:

- Functional: the task to accomplish
- Emotional: how they want to feel
- Social: how they want to be perceived

Most builders fixate on functional and miss the other two.

**Circumstances over attributes.** "35-year-old engineer" predicts nothing.
"Engineer who just inherited a legacy codebase" predicts a lot. Segment by
situation, not demographics.

**Hiring and firing.** Users hire your solution and fire the old way. What are
they firing? What switching costs make them hesitate? If you can't name what
they'd fire, the job might not exist.

## Using JTBD in Office Hours

When asking Q2 ("What job are they hiring this to do?"), push for:

1. A **verb** (not a noun) — doing, not having
2. An **outcome** in the user's language — not technical jargon
3. A **circumstance** that triggers the need — when does this matter?

If the answer sounds like a feature ("they need a notification system"), ask:
"When would they need to be notified, and what would they do with that
information?"
